## Stories to tell (pick 3–4, matched to their layers)

1. **We already have the diagnostic — behavioural, not self-report.** 50 of 57 people scored on a six-stage behavioural framework, evidenced from 115+ meeting transcripts and 1:1s, not surveys. Punchline: when we tightened evidence standards on Jun 30, 7 of 10 engineers' scores DROPPED — self-report and reality diverge, and we measure the reality. (Sets the bar their diagnostic has to clear.)
2. **The builder ladder.** 50% of the company has independently built tools in live use; 70% built or co-built at least one. Not course completions — named artifacts: a CFO's contract-obligation extraction that saved external legal fees, an underwriter's dashboard that took HubSpot note coverage 0→90%, a finance analyst's daily credit-control skill running 2+ months.
3. **What actually moves people: peer demos and unblocking, not content.** Jake's dashboard activated underwriting peers where workshops didn't; David Pilley went from our lowest stage to fluent when we fixed his Python install — the gap was environment, not knowledge. Company-wide sessions don't scale; we killed them. (Direct challenge to a courseware model.)
4. **Pods with mechanics.** Our version of Uber's Agentic Pods: PM defines, mechanics (the team's own Stage 2–3 people) build alongside the tech lead — the pod outlives the engagement because the domain experts can build. Vendor curricula teach *about* AI; our people ship tools into their own workflows.
5. **Measurement with consequences.** Capability feeds PGR (Growth pillar), hiring (AI-first interview being designed by one of our own engineers), and new-hire baselines. We're past "awareness training."

## Pointed questions (Scott for substance, Jack for commercials)

**The benchmark — our actual buying intent:**
1. How many organisations are in your benchmark pool, what sectors/sizes — and can you show us today where a ~60-person insurtech sits against peers? (This is the one thing we can't build ourselves.)
2. Can Athena ingest OUR framework — map its levels onto our six stages — or is it your rubric or nothing? We have 4 months of longitudinal data we won't discard.

**The diagnostic — test its substance:**
3. What does the diagnostic actually measure: self-assessment questionnaires, or observed behaviour/artifacts? What stops it inflating the way self-report always does?
4. Does your capability model distinguish *delegating whole tasks* from *orchestrating agent systems*? Where do your most advanced clients' engineers sit — and what does your top level look like in practice? (We have nobody at our Stage 5 and a defined path there; if their ceiling is "uses Copilot well," that's telling.)
5. What counts as an "adoption signal" in the Intelligence layer — logins and course completions, or artifacts in production use?

**The learning layer — the courseware trap:**
6. How much of the Knowledge layer is generic content vs built on OUR systems and workflows? Our evidence: in-workflow coached reps convert people; courses don't. How do you avoid being a content library with a dashboard?
7. Who keeps the frontier content current — model capabilities shift monthly; what shipped in the agentic-systems curriculum in the last 60 days?

**Assurance / commercial:**
8. The Assurance layer: what would "verifiable evidence of workforce capability" look like for an Admiral-group entity — and do you have an FS/insurance client of our size we can reference?
9. Pricing model — per-seat platform, one-off diagnostic, or programme? What's the smallest useful engagement? (Position: we'd pilot the diagnostic/benchmark on our existing baseline, not buy the full stack.)
