# Flock Communication Playbook

A guide to mastering presentations, panel discussions, and communication for maximum impact.

---

## Presentations

**Rule 01: Start at least a month early.** Three full iterations with a week between each. Starting a week before is the biggest mistake.

**Rule 02: Never go over 10 slides.**

**Rule 03: One point per slide. No exceptions.** Every element — visuals, numbers, quotes — must support one and only one argument.

**Rule 04: Every slide title must be a statement.** Not a topic. A complete sentence making a specific, arguable point.
- ❌ "Loss ratios" / "The effect of X on Y"
- ✅ "Loss ratios worsen when claims are reported late"

**Rule 05: The slide titles should tell the whole story.** Reading only the titles should reveal the complete argument from start to finish.

**Rule 06: Less content than you think. Always.** Cut it in half. Then cut it again. Audiences cannot simultaneously read and listen.

---

## Panel discussions

**Rule 01: Know the real exam question.** Spend 15 minutes with moderators beforehand to find the interesting angles, not the generic topics.

**Rule 02: Research everyone else on the panel.** Find commonalities, reference their points, position yourself thoughtfully.

**Rule 03: Prepare five points you will make.** Go in knowing five specific things you want the audience to remember regardless of what's asked.

**Rule 04: Master the pivot.**
- **"Can I tell you a story?"** — Most powerful phrase. Stops interruptions, reframes the conversation.
- **"Can I answer a subtly different question?"** — Acknowledge then redirect.
- **Disarm the room early** — Be upfront about gaps in your expertise.
- **Shape the agenda beforehand** — Email organizers with the angles you want.
- **If you don't know something — admit it strategically.** Never bluff. Use stories to redirect.

## The story method

### Why stories work

> "Go back to a conference five years later and someone will say 'you're the company that found that car in France.'" Generic statements disappear; real stories persist.

### Structure of every good story

1. **Beginning:** Real problem that occurred
2. **Middle:** How the problem was solved
3. **End:** Results and meaning

**Not a story:** "Data enables a much better understanding of customers and drives meaningful insights."

**A story:** "A customer crashed on the M1. We looked at the data — 99mph for 14 hours straight. We realised fatigue causes speed. We started flagging it. Claims dropped. Lives saved."

### Five hero stories required

Every team member needs five pre-packaged stories:
- **Safety stories** — data preventing incidents or saving drivers
- **Customer stories** — real customers with measurable impact
- **Technology stories** — what was built and its specific impact
- **Growth stories** — numbers proving the model (e.g. NPS 90 across 1,000 customers)

## Language

**Rule 01: Speak simply. Sound smarter.** The person who speaks most simply comes across as most authoritative. Clarity signals expertise, not jargon.

**Rule 02: Let customer data do the arguing.** "No one can argue with customers."
- Weak: "We think deployment will positively impact outcomes."
- Strong: "NPS 90 across 1,000 users. Customers agree — I don't need you to."

**Rule 03: Weave in authority naturally.** State accomplishments simply within context. Don't be arrogant; let facts land.

**Rule 04: Use "I think people will want to hear this."** Signals upcoming value; audiences lean in before the content lands.

---

## Final principle

> Simple, clear, compelling, backed by real data, no jargon. Applies to presentations, panels, investor meetings, interviews, and beyond.
