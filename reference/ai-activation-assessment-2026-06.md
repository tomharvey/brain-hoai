## Key Patterns & Strategic Observations

### 1. The activation target is within reach — but the path is assessment, not workshops

75% of the company is activated (Stage 2+) against Ed's 80% target. The gap is not that people aren't using AI — it's that 10 people haven't been assessed yet. If Christian Nielsen and even 2 of the underwriting ICs turn out to be Stage 2+, the target is met. The bottleneck is measurement, not enablement.

### 2. Engineering and Product have pulled away — every person at Stage 4+

All 10 engineers and all 4 product team members are at Stage 4 (Delegation) or above. This is the strongest cohort in the company by a wide margin. The gap is no longer "are they using AI?" but "can they propagate their methods to the rest of the company?" The June provocation session surfaced that even within engineering, methods aren't being shared — senior engineers are "not hating, but certainly not sharing."

### 3. The secretary → coach → strategic partner progression is real and repeatable

Adam Smith, Alex Dyball, and Matt Lees all independently developed AI-as-coach usage (meeting review, performance feedback, accountability) without prompting. This pattern appeared in all three distribution discovery calls. It is the progression that matters most for non-engineering roles and should be the central narrative for enablement.

### 4. Peer demos beat workshops for activation

Jake Wood (underwriting) and Shreya Chowta (ops) are the clearest examples: both were activated by seeing a peer use AI on a real problem, not by attending a workshop. Jake's entire underwriting rollout strategy is peer-led — "I'll be the little bridge over." The implication: invest in internal champions and peer pairing, not more company-wide sessions.

### 5. The "output ownership" failure mode is company-wide

Rob takes output at face value. David Pilley "cedes to AI" on errors. Adam Smith presented wrong numbers from Claude to SLT. Ed has "dangerously skip permissions" without the review muscle to know when to stop. This is the single most important cultural shift for Q3: **everyone owns the quality of what gets produced.**

### 6. Stage 4 is the new accumulation point

19 people sit at Stage 4 — the single largest cohort at 41% of scored people. Stage 3 has 9 people (20%). The Stage 4→5 transition is the next frontier for the top performers: moving from delegating tasks to directing systems with measured adherence. Ismael is the only person at Stage 5 — he should help define what that looks like for others.

### 7. Finance is stronger than the April baseline suggested

The April baseline had Finance at "Maturity: 3 with a Tier-5 outlier (Kirsty)." The June assessment shows Kirsty at Stage 4, Kevin and Ivan at Stage 3 (high confidence), and several others at Stage 2 with active use. The finance workshop (May 19) was a genuine catalyst. The remaining gap is the 3 unassessed people (Christian, David P, Pavel).

## Methodology

### Framework
All assessments use the [[activation-pathway]] framework (Stages 0–5). Stage definitions:
- **Stage 0** — Search mode: single prompt, no iteration
- **Stage 1** — First win: iterates, pushes back
- **Stage 2** — Context + tools: loads context, connects MCPs
- **Stage 3** — Fluency: argues with output, thinks with the model
- **Stage 4** — Delegation: delegates whole tasks, reviews at output level
- **Stage 5** — Orchestration: directs systems, measures adherence

**Activation threshold** sits at the top of Stage 1: 3 useful tasks in a context-fuelled Claude project.

### Evidence sources
- 112 meeting transcripts (March–June 2026)
- 48 people files with prior assessment notes
- 8 parallel research agents, each assigned a department
- Cross-department grep for every person's name and known aliases
- Name resolution applied per [[name-resolution.yaml]] to correct Granola transcription errors

### Confidence tiers
- **High**: 3+ independent sources, at least one direct observation
- **Medium**: 2 sources, or 1 strong 1:1 with direct evidence
- **Low**: 1 source only, or only third-party mentions
- **Unassessed**: no meaningful evidence — not scored

### Scoring principles
- Score **behaviour**, not understanding. A CEO who talks brilliantly about AI but doesn't use tools daily scores lower than an ops person who uses Claude every morning.
- Score **current practice in current context**, not historical peaks or other-company experience.
- Where existing assessment conflicts with new transcript evidence, the newer evidence wins. Conflicts are noted explicitly.
- "Unassessed" is an honest state. A low-confidence Stage 0 based on no evidence would be false precision.

### Known limitations
- No direct observation of people using tools — all evidence is self-reported or third-party described
- Transcripts are Granola recordings with known speech-to-text errors (corrected per name-resolution.yaml)
- Some March/April transcripts are stubs — meeting notes used as supplementary evidence
- 10 people remain unassessed — the gap is concentrated in underwriting and leadership
- Data staleness varies: some assessments draw on June 2026 evidence; others rely on April data
