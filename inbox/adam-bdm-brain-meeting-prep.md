# Adam Smith — BDM Brain intro

**Format:** 45 min, 1:1
**Goal:** Walk out with Adam's version of the problem, his read on the hypotheses, and his name on the pilot.

---

## What success looks like

At the end of this meeting Adam can describe — in his own words, without prompting — what the brain does for his team and why it matters for conversion. He has pointed at one or two hypotheses and said "yes, that one." He has agreed to be involved in shaping it.

Not success: Adam has heard a good presentation and nodded.

---

## Opening (5 min)

Don't open with the brain. Open with the problem.

> *"You've got four BDMs. Each of them has context the others don't. Every Monday morning, someone walks into a call not knowing a colleague spoke to that account last week. Every quarter, a deal goes cold and nobody noticed. That's not a people problem — it's a context problem."*
>
> *"Before I show you what we're building, I want to know which of those scenarios actually keeps you up at night — because that's where we start."*

Then stop talking. Let him answer.

---

## Questions to get answered (the whole meeting)

These are the things you cannot leave without:

1. **What's the specific moment where context fails him?** Not "generally" — a named situation. Pre-call? Post-meeting? When a deal goes quiet? Let him describe it.
2. **Of the candidate hypotheses, which one would change what he does tomorrow?** Read them out if needed — but ask him to pick, not to agree.
3. **Does he already have a word for ghost accounts?** If so, use his word.
4. **Does he expect to pull from the brain or have it push to him?** This tells you whether Phase 1 is sufficient or whether the proactive layer is what actually matters to him.
5. **Is this a tool for his team or a visibility tool for him?** Does he want to see what his BDMs are doing? Honest answer matters — it affects the design.
6. **Who on his team is the obvious first user?**

---

## What to show (only if needed to ground the conversation)

**The eight roles — in plain language, no architecture:**
> *"The brain can act as a librarian (what do we know about this account?), a secretary (you promised to follow up — did you?), a forecaster (this account has gone quiet — flag it before anyone notices), and a strategy partner (you're about to call them — here's what you know and what you should lead with)."*

Show only what the conversation needs. If he's already talking about the problem, don't interrupt with a framework.

**The "no new tool" frame — use if he looks uncertain:**
> *"This isn't a new tool. It talks through Claude, which you already use. It writes to Notion. It reads HubSpot. It can flag things in your inbox. It's an upgrade to what you're already doing, not something to learn."*

---

## What NOT to do

- Don't show the architecture
- Don't use "ontology", "MCP", "federated search", or "lint"
- Don't present the hypotheses as finished — present them as drafts he should argue with
- Don't let the meeting become a demo — the brain doesn't exist yet and that's fine

---

## What to leave open (he should own these)

- The final wording of the hypotheses — his language, not yours
- The vocabulary: what does his team call the stages, the statuses, the pipeline events?
- Which BDMs are in the pilot and in what order
- What "contributing to the brain" looks like day-to-day for his team

---

## What you're not deciding in this meeting

- Architecture, tooling, hosting — all Ollie's territory
- Feature scope and phase plan — Ollie's territory
- Workshop agenda — joint territory (Adam + Ollie meeting)
