## Key themes

### Ed's realisation: Web 3.0 → Web 4.0

Ed returned from paternity leave having used Codex heavily and with a new frame for where he is vs where he needs to be:

- **Web 3.0** (where Ed is): AI-augmented traditional tools. Using Notion, email, Slack with AI assistance on the side.
- **Web 4.0** (where Tom and Mima are): Living primarily in AI tools; occasionally venturing out to "neolithic" tools when forced to.

He crossed this chasm in a prior conversation with Mima — watching her work in Cursor powered by Claude Code and realising he'd missed the point entirely ("would you copy and paste that into Slack?" / "looking at me like I'm an idiot").

### Tom's personal OS demo

Walked Ed through the vault setup live:
- Granola transcripts auto-update initiatives, issues, and people files
- Name resolution layer corrects transcription errors (Harvey → [[javier]], gerund/Holly → [[geran]]/[[ollie-crowe]])
- People files populated from Bob screenshots (org chart) + meeting transcripts
- Files treated as binary data layer — Tom never reads them directly, queries the agent instead
- Primary input: typing in VS Code terminal (95%), not voice

Ed's reaction: "Holy. So it is just constantly building in real time in the background updating personnel files on what everyone is working on."

Key insight Tom surfaced: the product team's mistake is worrying about folder structure and document readability — "are these documents actually readable?" is the wrong question. The right question is "does my agent adhere to the context and give me what I need?"

### Vendor lock-in: Claude vs Codex

Ed raised concern about architectural lock-in with Anthropic given Codex momentum and OpenAI shipping 5 features/day. Tom's response:

- J is architected through Vercel as an abstraction layer — can switch models in theory
- Bedrock is a second abstraction layer between Flock and Anthropic
- Not locked in, but approaching a fork
- Anthropic is ~9 months ahead of OpenAI on business tools; just announced console on AWS
- The place most at risk of lock-in is **operational stuff / underwriting assistance** — not J
- Skills and MCPs should be portable; underwriting/finance process automation is where you'd feel a switch most
- Tom applied for o3 Codex preview "out of curiosity"
- Agreed: hold current position, don't push deeper into Anthropic-specific features without clear reason

Ed also noted: introducing Jordi to AI-centric pen-testing tool founders from his CEO community.

### Company-wide AI adoption: three cohorts + K curve

Ed independently described the same three cohorts Tom uses:

1. **Hardcore AGI-pilled**: Tom, Mima, Ollie, Ed (philosophically if not yet proficient)
2. **Hardcore naysayers**: Darren Nightingale, some finance team members
3. **Middle 80%**: interested, occasionally use AI, wish they were better

Ed shared the "K curve" framing from Mark (CEO, ScreenCloud): AI splits everyone onto the upper or lower arm of a K. The top 10% drag the 80% up or down; the bottom 10% need to exit.

Tom's nuance: within the middle 80%, there's a meaningful split — people who've found one resonant use case and chased it down (real gains, not paradigm shift) vs people who genuinely don't know where to go next. The former need bringing fully in; the latter need a first win.

### The activation threshold

Ed's proposed goal: **80% of the company crosses the activation threshold by end of June.**

Ed's activation threshold hypothesis: complete 3 useful tasks in a context-fuelled Claude project. Oddly specific is good — it forces concrete actions.

Tom's framing: the real blocker isn't use cases (people have those) — it's activation energy. People ask one question, get a mediocre answer, and move on. They're used to forms-with-submit-buttons. They need to learn how to have a conversation.

Agreed next step: define the threshold precisely, then plan rollout together.

### Tom as enabler, not centre of excellence

Ed's explicit framing — important:

> "People see you as an enabler, as an essential of enablement rather than a centre of excellence, which I think is really important. We need to be very careful about building an AI centre of excellence at Flock. We need everyone at Flock being like, Tom teaches me and enables me to do it rather than I'll go to Tom and he'll do it."

This validates the approach and gives air cover to scale through workshops rather than 1:1s.

### Flock o'clock workshops as the model

Ed wants to replicate the Flock o'clock format: bring laptops, Tom shows how it's done, everyone does it live in the room. Move from 1:1 pairing to group sessions. Ed wants to attend/co-run.

### The 1 ask: SLT owning AI outcomes

Tom surfaced the "1 ask" via the vault in the session. Ed's response: "I quite like it. It costs nothing — just a conversation." He endorsed it.

### Finance team

Ed's view: the "no time" excuse doesn't hold. "I was teaching myself Claude and attempting to automate as much of my life away as possible whilst closing an acquisition whilst my wife was eight months pregnant. I'm pretty sure the finance team who leave the office at 4:45..." Tom's response: he can block their calendars for AI time, but they also need to block their own — management needs to signal this matters.
