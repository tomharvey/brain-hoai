### Engineering maturity — the evolutionary leap

**The big question Fergus left Tom with:**

> "How do we teach them to fish? How do we get them into a space where they're not spending three months building a submissions pipeline?"

And then, after discussion:

> "Aligning around the state of the art. And helping the team that isn't there yet understand that there is a route through to what we're describing. Is something I think we should do. I don't know how to. That's an offer to you."

Key observations:

- **Submissions pipeline** has taken ~1 year (tracing back to Harvey and Amazon, 10–20% time). Core problem: scope set too large, nothing shipped early to iterate on. The volume of work is not wrong; the approach is.
- **Skills testing has been manual**, not automated — team not yet hitting harness problems, which means they're either not leaning on AI enough or using it poorly.
- **The next evolutionary step**: from "I use AI to help me write code" to "the AI is the code, it is the machine." Tom is already there; engineers are not — probably 2–3 months behind.
- [[alex-dyball|Alex]] asked Tom directly: "I get the sense that you think writing code is gone." Tom's internal response: "Yeah. I put it up with punch cards."
- [[rob|Rob]] still doing some hands-on polish after AI output — noted but not alarming. Fergus: "If they said they haven't reviewed code in months, then I'd be worried."
- **Retro on submissions pipeline won't work** — "why the fuck is there no build in a week?" won't produce a constructive conversation. Better: align around the state of the art, show the team there's a route through.
