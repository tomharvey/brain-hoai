## Key themes

### PACE system — too complex to extract

- PACE is 15–20 interconnected skills; they all depend on each other (Slack, Granola, Notion input tracking layer)
- Without that layer the system has no data to track goals against
- Can't extract a single skill without the full architecture
- Not suitable for rolling out directly to the company

### Minimum viable slice

- Consensus: a single skill that ingests existing inputs (Slack, HubSpot, Gmail) and maps them to PGR goals
- Not a new process, just a layer on top of what already happens
- **Key design decision**: tool is better aimed at the employee, not the manager
  - Manager-facing version puts pressure on managers to set and track goals
  - Employee-facing version makes individuals responsible for their own development
- Francesco's use case as the model: weekly Notion doc, updated against goals, ready to bring to 1:1s
  - Runs every Monday, surfaces how the week's work maps to each goal
  - Example: "presented to pricing committee" flagged as evidence for "influencing senior stakeholders"
- Ollie's friction point: tool generating too much pre-1:1 prep, crowding out organic conversation
  - Fix: surface the 5 things that most contributed to goals and 3 that distracted

### PGR rollout approach

- Rakhee wants it as an optional part of PGR, not a company-wide mandate
- Original fairness concern ("how do we make this fair?") is a people-team problem, not a tool problem
- Tool doesn't make evaluation more or less fair — that's about how the framework is defined
- Better framing: get commitment from a small group rather than a vague opt-in nobody uses
- Focus group of 5–6 people across different teams was the original intent
- Incentive is real: bonuses tied to performance, not just promotion

### Role of the people team

- Rakhee and [[eraaz-ali|Eraaz]] are rewriting the performance review process — they should own the design
- Francesco and Ollie happy to support orchestration; people team should drive the "what"
- If Rakhee asks "how do we make this fair?" — that's a people-team problem
