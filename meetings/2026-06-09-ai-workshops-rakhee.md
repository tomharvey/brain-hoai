## Key themes

### Workshop structure for the rest of the year

[[rakhee]] wants workshops mapped out for the rest of the year with structure and continuity — not one-offs that are forgotten. Tom agrees, but notes company-wide workshops alone won't get underwriters onto AI.

**Split model confirmed:**
- Company-wide: good for topics like co-working sessions (Ivan can join). Keeps broad momentum.
- Team-based: surfaces team-specific use cases more effectively. The small team count makes this low-cost for Tom. The team-based ones are where things actually surface — better conversations, more specific problems.

### Underwriter adoption

[[jake-wood]] has started pushing AI onto Mat and Curtis (as of the Friday before this call). Belly (colourblind; see name flags) also starting to use it. Strategy: break the floodgate, then push individually, then run team sessions. Tom needs to keep nudging Jake along — let Tom feel the problems before solving them for underwriters.

### AI activation framework

Current "everyone has written a skill" approach is process-focused, not outcome-focused. Tom pitching a new 4-level activation framework:

1. Asking basic questions
2. Understanding tools and context flow
3. Full conversations to think through problems
4. Goal-directed: telling the machine the goal and letting it push back

Not everyone needs to reach level 4. Reasonable goals should be set per level, applied across all teams with team-specific ways of meeting each level. Designed to answer Ed's mandate in a structured, measurable way. → [[AI-107]]

### Messaging and culture — Ed's stance vs. Rakhee's

Ed's "AI-first company" mandate is owned by the people team. Rakhee disagrees:
- This is a company-wide philosophy, not a people team remit
- Focus should be on enabling and supporting, not scoring and surveying
- The AI benchmark survey (done 2025) may recur, but scores alone don't capture progress
- Ed's message is right in intent but lands as "you're behind, catch up" rather than building knowledge and confidence

**Shift for next quarter**: move from "just get your hands dirty" to "find the right use case." Two reasons: (1) some people haven't engaged because they haven't found a relevant use case; (2) some are tiring of the noise. Apple's WWDC conference modeled the right approach: hyper-specific, customer-first, no superintelligence talk.

### Job security concerns — opening the space

Tom has started being open to people voicing deeper anxieties. View: a "bloodbath" in job markets is coming for the industry, but Flock's 70 people could double their salaries if they get ahead of it. Framing AI as an opportunity to be on the right side of the change.

[[alex-dyball]] quote: "There's this huge change coming and I feel very lucky to be at a company that is helping us get to the other side of it." Tom's framing: that's exactly the standpoint, as opposed to "AI is going to take everyone's job."

People are scared — not just of AI but of what the Admiral acquisition means on top. Some are worried about job security even though they shouldn't be. The opportunity framing (like Alex Dyball's) is the antidote.

### Admiral want to piggyback

Admiral feel behind on AI and want to piggyback on Flock's workshops. Rakhee received that message directly. Tom takes on the "leave it with me" on that front.
